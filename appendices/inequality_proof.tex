\chapter{Non-Unique Ordering of Single-Leak Equivalents in a Three-Leak Pipe}
\label{app:3_leak_eq_proof}

This appendix shows that, for a pipe with three leaks, the location of the corresponding single-leak equivalent does not necessarily preserve a fixed ordering relative to the actual leaks.\\

Consider a straight pipeline of length $\ell$ operating in steady state, with three leaks located at
\[
0<z_1<z_2<z_3<\ell,
\]
and following the leak model presented in \autoref{chap:model}. Since each leak removes flow from the pipe,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:leak_ordering}
0<q_{\ell}<q_2<q_1<q_0.
\end{equation}

The total pressure drop across the pipe is equal to the sum of the pressure drop of each non-leaking pipe segment:
\begin{align}
h_0-h_{\ell}
&=
\theta\left(
L_1q_0^2
+L_2q_1^2
+L_3q_2^2
+(\ell-L_1-L_2-L_3)q_{\ell}^2
\right) \notag \\
&=
\theta\left(
\ell q_{\ell}^2
+L_1(q_0^2-q_{\ell}^2)
+L_2(q_1^2-q_{\ell}^2)
+L_3(q_2^2-q_{\ell}^2)
\right).
\label{eq:three_leaks_pres_drop}
\end{align}

Let $L_{eq}$ denote the location of a fictitious single leak that produces the same pressure drop and inlet-outlet flows. For this single-leak equivalent,
\begin{align}
h_0-h_{\ell}
&=
\theta\left(
L_{eq}q_0^2
+(\ell-L_{eq})q_{\ell}^2
\right) \notag \\
&=
\theta\left(
\ell q_{\ell}^2
+L_{eq}(q_0^2-q_{\ell}^2)
\right).
\label{eq:single_leak_eq}
\end{align}

Equating \cref{eq:three_leaks_pres_drop} and \cref{eq:single_leak_eq} gives
\begin{equation*}
L_{eq}
=
L_1
+L_2r_1
+L_3r_2,
\end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*}
r_1
:=
\dfrac{q_1^2-q_{\ell}^2}{q_0^2-q_{\ell}^2},
\qquad
r_2
:=
\dfrac{q_2^2-q_{\ell}^2}{q_0^2-q_{\ell}^2}.
\end{equation*}
By \cref{eq:leak_ordering},
\begin{equation}
0<r_2<r_1<1.
\label{eq:ratio_bounds}
\end{equation}

To determine the position of the equivalent leak relative to the second leak, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Delta_r}
\Delta
:=
L_{eq}-z_2
=
L_{eq}-(L_1+L_2)
=
-L_2(1-r_1)+L_3r_2.
\end{equation}
Hence,
\begin{equation*}
\Delta<0
\iff
L_{eq}\in(z_1,z_2),
\qquad
\Delta>0
\iff
L_{eq}\in(z_2,z_3).
\end{equation*}

The boundary between the two cases is
\begin{equation*}
r_2
=
\dfrac{L_2}{L_3}(1-r_1).
\end{equation*}

Both signs of $\Delta$ are compatible with the admissible ordering in \cref{eq:ratio_bounds}. First, fix any $r_1\in(0,1)$ and choose
\begin{equation*}
0<r_2<
\min\left(
r_1,\,
\dfrac{L_2}{L_3}(1-r_1)
\right).
\end{equation*}
Then $0<r_2<r_1<1$, and \cref{eq:Delta_r} gives
\begin{equation*}
\Delta
=
-L_2(1-r_1)+L_3r_2
<
-L_2(1-r_1)+L_3\dfrac{L_2}{L_3}(1-r_1)
=
0.
\end{equation*}
Thus, $L_{eq}\in(z_1,z_2)$.\\

Conversely, choose $r_1$ such that
\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{L_2}{L_2+L_3}<r_1<1.
\end{equation*}
Then, by rearranging the lower bound, $\dfrac{L_2}{L_3}(1-r_1)<r_1$, so one can choose
\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{L_2}{L_3}(1-r_1)<r_2<r_1.
\end{equation*}
Again $0<r_2<r_1<1$, but now \cref{eq:Delta_r} gives
\begin{equation*}
\Delta
=
-L_2(1-r_1)+L_3r_2
>
-L_2(1-r_1)+L_3\dfrac{L_2}{L_3}(1-r_1)
=
0.
\end{equation*}
Thus, $L_{eq}\in(z_2,z_3)$.\\

Therefore, there exist admissible flow-ratio pairs for which the single-leak equivalent lies upstream of the second leak, and there exist admissible flow-ratio pairs for which it lies downstream of the second leak. Consequently, the ordering of the actual leaks and the single-leak equivalent is not unique across all steady-state operating points for a pipe with three leaks.

\begin{center}
    \noindent\fbox{
        \parbox{0.9\textwidth}{
            The sign of $\Delta=L_{eq}-z_2$ is not fixed by the ordering of the three physical leaks, but depends on the operating point through $r_1$ and $r_2$. Hence, a three-leak pipe does not admit a unique global ordering between the physical leaks and their single-leak equivalent.
        }
    }
\end{center}

This sign change also appears in simulated steady-state measurements. Consider the pipe
\[
\ell=1,
\qquad
\theta=0.4,
\]
with
\begin{align*}
z_1&=0.2,
&
z_2&=0.5,
&
z_3&=0.8,\\
c_1&=0.015,
&
c_2&=0.010,
&
c_3&=0.020.
\end{align*}
The following two simulated measurement vectors give opposite signs of $\Delta$:
\begin{align*}
y^{(1)}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
10 & 1 & 9.65 & 0.86
\end{bmatrix}^{\top}
&\implies
\Delta = 0.02>0,
\\
y^{(2)}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
10 & 5 & 0.20 & 4.91
\end{bmatrix}^{\top}
&\implies
\Delta = -0.04<0.
\end{align*}
Thus, the first operating point places the equivalent leak downstream of $z_2$, while the second places it upstream of $z_2$. The simulations, therefore, provide a concrete example of the non-unique ordering established above.
