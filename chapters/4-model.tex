\chapter{Pipe and Leak Model}
\label{chap:model}

This chapter derives the mathematical model for a leaking pipe system that forms the basis for the work presented in this thesis. It begins with the transient one-dimensional flow equations for pressurized pipe systems and derives the equations introduced in \autoref{sec:task_desc}. The chapter then derives a set of recursive equations that describe the system's steady-state dynamics and are used to develop the leak identification method.

\section{Modeling of Pipe With Leaks}

Under the assumption that water in a closed straight pipe flows one-dimensionally, so that the flow variables are approximated by their cross-sectional averages, and the material of the pipe is linearly elastic\footnote{Linearly elastic means that stress is proportional to strain and is true for most pipe materials such as metal, concrete, and wood \cite[p.~27]{chaudhry2013}.}, according to \cite[pp.~27--33]{chaudhry2013}, the momentum and continuity equations describe transient hydraulic behavior:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:momentum}
\frac{1}{gA}\frac{\partial Q(z,t)}{\partial t}
+ \frac{\partial H(z,t)}{\partial z}
+ \theta Q^{2}(z,t) = 0
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:continuity}
\frac{\partial H(z,t)}{\partial t}
+ \frac{a^{2}}{gA}\frac{\partial Q(z,t)}{\partial z} = 0
\end{equation}

$Q(z,t)\;[m^3/s]$ and $H(z,t)\;[m]$ are the flow and head that vary both by the location along the pipe (\gls{z}) and time ($t$). Head is defined as
\[
H = \frac{P}{\rho g} + z_e,
\]
where $P$ is the fluid pressure, $\rho$ is the fluid density, and $z_e$ is the elevation above a chosen datum. It represents the mechanical energy per unit weight of fluid and corresponds to the height to which the fluid would rise in a piezometer connected to the pipe. Spatial and temporal variations of $h$ therefore describe pressure surges and depressions during hydraulic transients.\\

Furthermore, $A$ is the pipe cross-sectional area, $g$ is gravitational acceleration, and $a$ is the pressure wave speed accounting for fluid compressibility and pipe wall elasticity. 
The resistance coefficient \gls{theta} accounts for quasi-steady friction losses and is given by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:theta_defined}
\theta = \frac{f}{2 g D A^{2}},
\end{equation}
with \gls{dwfric} being the Darcy--Weisbach friction factor and $D$ the pipe diameter.\\

A leak in the pipe is modeled as a valve with a constant opening. Following \cite[pp.~54]{chaudhry2013}, the flow through a valve, denoted by $\tilde{q}$, at location $z^*$,can be expressed as
\begin{equation*}
\tilde{q}(t) = C_d A_v \sqrt{2 g \, h(z^*,t)},
\end{equation*}
where $C_d$ is the discharge coefficient and $A_v$ is the effective area of the valve opening.  \\

A leak located at position $z^*$ can therefore be modeled by evaluating the valve flow relation at that location, yielding
\begin{equation}
\tilde{q}(t) = c \sqrt{h(z^*,t)},
\label{eq:leak_model}
\end{equation}
where $c = C_d A_v \sqrt{2 g}$ is a constant parameter that characterizes the size and hydraulic properties of the leak.\\

A pipe with \gls{n} leaks can be modeled as $n+1$ sections of non-leaking pipes following \cref{eq:momentum,eq:continuity} with boundary conditions defined by considering the relation between head and flow both upstream and downstream of a leak at $z^*$:

\begin{align}
    \lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow0} h(z^*-\epsilon,t) &= \lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow0} h(z^*+\epsilon,t) \label{eq:head_boundary} \\
    \lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow0} q(z^*-\epsilon,t) &= \lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow0} q(z^*+\epsilon,t) + \tilde{q}(t) \label{eq:flow_boundary}
\end{align}

\subsection*{Extension to Non-Straight Pipes}
It should be noted that the mathematical formulation in \cref{eq:momentum,eq:continuity} assumes a straight pipe. This does not limit generality, since a non-straight pipeline can be modeled as a combination of straight segments connected by non-straight elements (fittings), whose effect can be represented through an equivalent straight length (ESL). The ESL is defined as the length of an ideal straight pipe, with the same characteristics as the real system, that produces an identical pressure drop. \\

This equivalence is obtained by accounting for the additional losses introduced by each fitting between straight pipeline segments. Using the Darcy--Weisbach equation \cite[pp.~238--242]{mott2006}, the ESL of the pipeline, $\ell_e$, is given by
\begin{equation*}
\ell_e = \ell_s + \frac{D}{f}\sum_{i=1}^{n_f} K_i
\end{equation*}
where $\ell_s$ is the total length of the straight pipe sections, $D$ is the pipe diameter, \gls{dwfric} is the Darcy--Weisbach friction factor, $K_i$ is the loss coefficient of the $i$-th fitting (usually provided by manufacturers), and $n_f$ is the total number of fittings in the pipeline.

\section{Derivation of Steady-State Equations}

In a steady-state, time derivatives are zero:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:zero_derivatives}
    \frac{\partial Q(z,t)}{\partial t}=0, \quad \frac{\partial H(z,t)}{\partial t}=0,
\end{equation}
and the head and flow are no longer time varying:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:steady_state}
    \lim_{t\rightarrow\infty} H(z,t)=h(z), \quad \lim_{t\rightarrow\infty} Q(z,t)=q(z).
\end{equation}
Combining \cref{eq:momentum,eq:continuity} with \cref{eq:zero_derivatives,eq:steady_state} gives the steady state equations for a non-leaking pipe segment:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:momentum_ss}
\frac{d h(z)}{d z}
= -\theta q^{2}(z),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:continuity_ss}
\frac{d q(z)}{d z} = 0.
\end{equation}
Comparing \cref{eq:momentum_ss} with \cref{eq:task_desc_ufunc}, it follows from this derivation that the head loss function equates to
\[
    \text{\gls{U}} (q(z)) = \theta q^{2}(z)
\]
Consider a non-leaking pipe section between the leaks $z_i$ and $z_{i+1}$. It follows from \cref{eq:continuity_ss} that the flow $q_i$ is constant along the entire section. Utilizing the fundamental theorem of calculus to solve \cref{eq:momentum_ss},  the change of head along the non-leaking section is given by
\begin{align}
    \int_{z_i}^{z_{i+1}} \frac{d h(z)}{d z} dz &= \int_{z_i}^{z_{i+1}} -\theta q_i^{2} dz  \notag \\
    h(z_{i+1}) - h(z_i) &= -\theta q_i^{2} (z_{i+1}-z_i) \notag \\ 
    h_i &= h_{i+1} + \theta L_{i+1} q_i^{2} \label{eq:head_change}.
\end{align}
From \cref{eq:leak_model,eq:head_boundary}, it follows that the flow through a leak is given by
\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{q}_i = c_i\sqrt{h_i}.
\end{equation*}
Recall that \gls{ci} is the discharge coefficient for this specific leak. The change of flow in the pipe can be determined from \cref{eq:flow_boundary} and is
\begin{equation} \label{eq:flow_change}
    q_{i} = q_{i+1}+\tilde{q}_{i+1}.
\end{equation}
\cref{eq:head_change,eq:flow_change} expresses head and flow by the downstream leak. By shifting the index $i$ and rearranging, $h_i$ and $q_i$ can be expressed by the upstream leak. In this work, the upstream equations will be used, which are summarized below.

\begin{samepage}
\section{Summary}
Given a pipe operating in steady state, and under the assumptions previously stated, the following equations and \autoref{fig:leak_notation_summary} describe the system around a leak at location \gls{zi}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Piezometric Head:}  
    \begin{align}
        \text{\gls{hi}} &= h_{i-1} - \text{\gls{theta}} \text{\gls{Li}} q_{i-1}^2 \label{eq:upstream_head}
    \end{align}

    \item \textbf{Leak Flow:}  
    \begin{equation}
        \text{\gls{tildeqi}} = \text{\gls{ci}}\sqrt{h_i} \label{eq:leak_flow}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Flow in Non-Leaking Segment:}  
    \begin{align}
        \text{\gls{qi}} &= q_{i-1}-\tilde{q}_{i} \notag \\
        &= q_{i-1} - \text{\gls{ci}}\sqrt{h_i} \label{eq:upstream_flow}
    \end{align}
\end{enumerate}
