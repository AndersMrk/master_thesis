\chapter{Theory}
\label{chap:theory}

This chapter introduces the theoretical background for the methods developed and the analysis performed in this work. It begins by defining nonlinear residual problems and presenting fundamental concepts related to the local structure and sensitivity of the residual function.\\

The chapter then introduces iterative optimization methods for solving nonlinear least-squares problems. The chapter concludes with a detailed presentation of the Levenberg--Marquardt method, which forms the foundation of the solver adopted in this work.

\section{Nonlinear Residual Problems}

Let 
\[
r : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m
\]
be a nonlinear and continuously differentiable function. The nonlinear residual problem is to find any
\begin{equation*}
     x^\ast \in \mathbb{R}^n \quad \text{such that} \quad r(x^*) = 0.
\end{equation*}
The function $r(x)$ is referred to as the \emph{residual} function.

\subsection{The Jacobian Matrix}

The Jacobian of \(r\) at a point \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is the matrix
\[
J(x) = \frac{\partial r(x)}{\partial x}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\dfrac{\partial r_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial r_1}{\partial x_n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\dfrac{\partial r_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial r_m}{\partial x_n}
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}.
\]

Using first-order Taylor expansion, the Jacobian represents the first-order linearization of \(r\) near \(x\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:jac_res_approx}    
r(x + \Delta x) \approx r(x) + J(x)\,\Delta x.
\end{equation}

Thus, \(J(x)\) describes the local sensitivity of the residual with respect to perturbations in the variables.

\subsection{Existence and Number of Local Solutions}
\label{sec:sol_existence}

Let $x^\ast \in \mathbb{R}^n$ satisfy $r(x^\ast)=0$, and assume that $r$ is continuously differentiable in a neighborhood of $x^\ast$. The local behavior of the solution set is closely related to the rank of the Jacobian $J(x^\ast)$, provided the rank is constant in a neighborhood of $x^\ast$.

\subsubsection*{Case $m = n$ (square system)}

Suppose $m=n$ such that $J(x^\ast)$ is square. If the Jacobian is nonsingular,
\[
\det J(x^\ast) \neq 0,
\]
then, by the Inverse Function Theorem, $r$ is locally invertible near $x^\ast$. Consequently, there exists a neighborhood around $x^\ast$ in which no other solutions exist, and $x^\ast$ is a locally unique solution \cite[pp.~657-660]{lee2013smooth}.\\

If $J(x^\ast)$ is singular, the local behavior of the solution set cannot be determined from first-order information alone. In this case, the solution may fail to be isolated, and additional solutions may exist arbitrarily close to $x^\ast$.

\subsubsection*{Case $m < n$ (underdetermined system)}


If $J(x^\ast)$ has full row rank $m$, then the Regular Level Set Theorem implies that the solution set near $x^\ast$ is not isolated. Instead, it forms a smooth manifold of dimension $n - m$ \cite[pp.~105-106]{lee2013smooth}. Hence, there are infinitely many nearby solutions, parameterized
by $n - m$ degrees of freedom.

\subsubsection*{Case $m > n$ (overdetermined system)}

In an overdetermined system, the number of equations exceeds the number of unknowns. Such systems are often inconsistent, meaning that no exact solution exists, and is a motivation for the least-squares formulations as presented in \autoref{sec:theory_optmet} \cite[p.~37]{beck2014nonlinearoptimization}.\\

Suppose, however, that a point $x^\ast$ satisfies $r(x^\ast)=0$. If $J(x^\ast)$ has full column rank $n$, then some square subsystem has a nonsingular Jacobian. The Inverse Function Theorem applied to that subsystem implies that $x^\ast$ is locally isolated \cite[pp.~657-660]{lee2013smooth}. \\

If $\operatorname{rank} J(x^\ast)<n$, first-order information alone does not determine the local structure, and the solution may be isolated or non-isolated depending on higher-order terms.

\subsubsection*{Global Solution}

The Jacobian $J(x^\ast)$ characterizes only the \emph{local} structure of the solution set. Even if $J(x^\ast)$ is nonsingular and $x^\ast$ is locally unique, the nonlinear system may possess additional solutions elsewhere in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Consequently, local invertibility of the Jacobian does not imply global uniqueness \cite[pp.~657-660]{lee2013smooth}. \\

Global existence or uniqueness of a solution to $r(x)=0$ requires additional structural assumptions on $r$, which go beyond the relevant scope of this work. In the absence of such properties, the existence and uniqueness of a solution cannot be guaranteed from an analysis of the Jacobian.

\section{Local Sensitivity of the Residual}

The Jacobian matrix characterizes how perturbations in the variables locally influence the residual, as shown in \cref{eq:jac_res_approx}. The magnitude of this influence may vary significantly depending on the direction of perturbation. Understanding these directional effects is important for assessing how reliably the variables can be inferred from the residual. This can be analyzed through the singular values of the Jacobian.\\

\subsection{Singular Value Decomposition}

Let $J \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ denote the Jacobian evaluated at a point of interest. Its singular value decomposition (SVD) \cite[pp.~41-42]{bjork2008} is
\begin{equation*}
J = U \Sigma V^\top
\end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*}
U =
\begin{bmatrix}
    u_1 & \cdots & u_m
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m},
\quad
V =
\begin{bmatrix}
    v_1 & \cdots & v_n
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}
\end{equation*}
are orthogonal matrices, where $v_j \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $u_j \in \mathbb{R}^m$ are regarded as left and right singular vectors. The matrix \(\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) contains the singular values and is diagonal with the block form
\begin{align*}
    \Sigma &=
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \Sigma_p & 0\\
        0 & 0
    \end{bmatrix}
    \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n},\\
    \Sigma_p &= \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\dots,\sigma_p),
\end{align*}
where \(p=\min(m,n)\). The singular values are ordered as
\begin{equation*}
\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge \sigma_p \ge 0.
\end{equation*}

From \cref{eq:jac_res_approx} and letting $\Delta x=\delta v_j$ be a small perturbation along a right singular vector:
\begin{align*}
    r(x+\delta v_j) & \approx r(x) + J(x)\delta v_j\\
    & = r(x) + \delta U \Sigma V^\top v_j \\
    & = r(x) + \delta \sigma_ju_j.
\end{align*}

Hence, a perturbation along $v_j$ in the variable space approximates a direction of change $u_j$ in the residual space, scaled by the corresponding singular value, $\sigma_j$. The singular values are therefore a measure of local sensitivity, where the residual is most sensitive to variable changes in $v_1$, and least sensitive to changes in $v_p$. If one or more singular values are close to zero, the Jacobian is nearly rank-deficient, indicating directions where the residual provides little local information about the variables.

\subsection{Condition Number of the Jacobian}
A scalar measure of local sensitivity is given by the condition number of the Jacobian,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:cond_num_def}
\kappa(J) = \frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_p},
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_1$ and $\sigma_p$ respectively denote the largest and smallest singular values of $J$ \cite[pp.~131-134]{bjork2008}.\\

The condition number summarizes the spread of the singular values. If they are of comparable magnitude, the condition number is small, meaning that perturbations in different variable directions produce residual changes of similar size. A large condition number indicates directions where the residual changes comparatively weakly. In such directions, small residual perturbations may correspond to large changes in the variables, reducing numerical stability. The problem is then considered \emph{ill-conditioned}.\\

A large condition number at the solution indicates that the solution space of the residual is locally flat in at least one direction. Points near and in flat directions may therefore yield similar residual values, making it harder for a solver to converge towards the true solution.\\

It is important to note that the condition number is a property of the function $r(x)$, not of the algorithm used to evaluate it. An ill-conditioned problem is therefore intrinsically difficult to solve accurately. Even with exact input data, rounding errors introduced by floating-point arithmetic may be amplified and lead to large perturbations in the final result \cite[p.~133]{bjork2008}.

\section{Optimization Methods}
\label{sec:theory_optmet}

In practice, it is often hard to solve a nonlinear residual problem directly. Instead, the problem can be reformulated as a nonlinear least squares optimization problem with the scalar objective function
\begin{equation}
f(x) = \frac{1}{2} \| r(x) \|^2 \in \mathbb{R}.
\label{eq:least_squares_objective}
\end{equation}
Solving the minimization problem,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:min_problem}
    x^\ast = \underset{x}{\text{argmin}} \ f(x) = \underset{x}{\text{argmin}} \ \frac{1}{2} \| r(x) \|^2,
\end{equation}
satisfies $r(x^*) = 0$ if an exact solution exists. If no exact solution exists, or if the system is overdetermined, the formulation yields a solution that minimizes the residual norm.\\

Solving \cref{eq:min_problem} is generally very hard \cite{madsen2004}, since there might exist local minima that are larger than a global minima. Instead, many methods aim to solve the local problem of finding $x^\ast \in \mathbb{R}^n$ such that
\begin{equation*}
    f(x^*) \leq f(x) \ \text{for} \ \|x-x^*\|<\delta,
\end{equation*}
where $\delta$ is some small positive number.\\

A group of methods used to solve the local optimization problem operates by iteratively evaluating the objective function at the current iterate $x_k$ and determining a step $s_k$ that decreases the cost function. The next iterate is computed as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:step_update_rule}
    x_{k+1} = x_k + s_k,
\end{equation}
with the goal of choosing $s_k$ so that
\begin{equation*}
    f(x_{k+1}) < f(x_k).
\end{equation*}
There are two main frameworks for controlling how the step $s_k$ is chosen, called \textit{Line Search} and \textit{Trust Region} methods. This section will introduce these frameworks along with common algorithms used to solve the minimization problem.

\subsection{Line Search Methods}

Line search methods \cite{madsen2004} determine the step $s_k$ by selecting a search direction $p_k$ and a scalar step length $\alpha > 0$. The step is written as
\begin{equation*}
s_k = \alpha p_k.
\end{equation*}

At each iteration, a direction $p_k$ is first computed using a chosen optimization method. The step length $\alpha$ is then selected such that the objective function decreases,
\[
f(x_k + \alpha p_k) < f(x_k).
\]

To analyze the choice of $\alpha$, the multivariate objective function is reduced to a one-dimensional function along the search direction,
\begin{equation}
\phi(\alpha) = f(x_k + \alpha p_k).
\end{equation}
The step length selection then corresponds to minimizing $\phi(\alpha)$ for $\alpha > 0$ \cite{madsen2004}. In principle, the optimal step length is given by
\[
\alpha^\ast = \underset{\alpha > 0}{\operatorname{argmin}} \ \phi(\alpha).
\]

In practice, computing the exact minimizer of $\phi(\alpha)$ is rarely feasible. The function may contain multiple local minima, and evaluating $\phi(\alpha)$ requires computing the objective function itself. \autoref{fig:line_search} shows an example of a situation where finding the global minimizer is difficult. As a result, determining $\alpha^\ast$ would require solving a separate optimization problem at each iteration. Line search, therefore, becomes an iterative procedure, where candidate values of $\alpha$ are evaluated until a step length that sufficiently reduces the objective function is found.\\

    \caption{Example of a cost function along a search direction $p_k$, where $\alpha$ is the step lenght along the search direction.}
    \label{fig:line_search}
\end{figure}

Practical line search algorithms aim to find a step length that provides a sufficient decrease rather than the exact minimizer. Common strategies include Armijo backtracking and step selection based on Wolfe conditions. These methods impose conditions on the decrease of the objective function and on the gradient along the search direction. A detailed introduction to these techniques is not required for the work presented in this report and is therefore omitted.



\subsection{Gradient Descent}
Gradient descent \cite{madsen2004} is a simple method that is based on moving in the direction of the negative gradient of the objective function. 
Using the chain rule, the gradient of $f$ is given by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gradient_f}
    \nabla f(x) = J(x)^\top r(x),
\end{equation}
where $J(x)$ denotes the Jacobian of the residual function.\\

At the current iterate $x_k$, the search direction is chosen as the direction of steepest descent:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:steepest_descent}
    p_k = - \nabla f(x_k) = - J(x_k)^\top r(x_k).
\end{equation}

Gradient descent is simple to implement and requires only first-order derivative information, provided by the Jacobian. It is computationally inexpensive per iteration and applicable even when second-order derivatives are unavailable. However, the method may converge slowly, especially for ill-conditioned problems or narrow curved valleys in the objective function. Its performance is sensitive to scaling and may require many iterations to achieve high accuracy.

\subsection{Newton's Method}

Newton's method \cite{madsen2004} incorporates second-order information of the objective function. 
The gradient of $f$ is given by \cref{eq:gradient_f} and the Hessian is given by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hessian}
    \nabla^2 f(x) = J(x)^\top J(x) + \sum_{i=1}^m r_i(x)\nabla^2 r_i(x).
\end{equation}

At iteration $k$, the Newton search direction is obtained by solving the linear system
\begin{equation*}
    \nabla^2 f(x_k) p_k = - \nabla f(x_k).
\end{equation*}

By incorporating curvature information, Newton's method typically achieves quadratic convergence in a neighborhood of a solution, provided that the Hessian is nonsingular. This represents a significant improvement over gradient descent in terms of local convergence speed.\\

However, the method requires computing the Hessian matrix, which can be computationally expensive or infeasible for some problems. Moreover, if the Hessian is indefinite or poorly conditioned, the method may fail to produce a descent direction without additional safeguards.

\subsection{Gauss--Newton Method}

The Gauss--Newton method \cite{madsen2004} is tailored specifically to nonlinear least squares problems. 
It is based on an approximation of \cref{eq:hessian},
\begin{equation*}
    \nabla^2 f(x) \simeq J(x)^\top J(x),
\end{equation*}
which neglects the second-order term involving $\nabla^2 r_i(x)$.\\

The search direction is therefore computed by solving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gauss_newton}
    J(x_k)^\top J(x_k) p_k = - J(x_k)^\top r(x_k).
\end{equation}

This approximation avoids explicit computation of second derivatives and reduces computational cost compared to full Newton's method. When the residual is small and near the solution, the neglected term becomes insignificant, and the method exhibits fast local convergence.\\

The Gauss--Newton method may perform poorly when the residual is large or when $J(x_k)^\top J(x_k)$ is rank deficient. In such cases, the Hessian approximation can be inaccurate, and additional regularization techniques may be required to ensure robustness.

\subsection{Trust Region Methods}
\label{sec:trust_region_methods}

Trust-region methods determine the step $s_k$ by minimizing a local model $m_k$ of the objective function within a restricted neighborhood around the current iterate. From \cite{madsen2004}, a typical model is the second order taylor expansion, using the Gauss--Newton approximation of the Hessian, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mk}
f(x_k+s) \simeq m_k(s) = f(x_k) + r(x_k)^\top J(x_k) \, s + \frac{1}{2} s^\top J(x_k)^\top J(x_k) \, s
\end{equation}

In the trust region method, it is assumed that there exists a radius $\Delta$ around $x_k$, such that the model $m_k(s)$ is sufficiently accurate within the region. The step is then computed by solving the constrained minimization problem,

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:trust_region_subproblem}
    s_k = \underset{s}{\text{argmin}} \ m_k(s) \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|s\| \leq \Delta_k.
\end{equation}

In practice, this is solved through solving a damped problem, where the step is computed by solving the unconstrained minimization problem,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:damped_problem}
    s_k = \underset{s}{\text{argmin}} \ \left(m_k(s) + \frac{1}{2} \ \lambda \ s^\top s\right),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \geq 0$ is the damping parameter, and $\lambda \ s^\top s$ penalizes large steps. \\

For a given $\Delta$ or $\lambda$, if no sufficient step $s_k$ is found such that $f(x_k+s_k)<f(x_k)$ (assuming $x_k$ is not a solution), the parameters ($\Delta,\lambda$) must be tuned such that a sufficient step is found. Since $m_k(s)$ is assumed to be a sufficient approximation for $f(x_k+s)$ when $s$ is sufficiently small, the trust region should be decreased ($\lambda$ increased) for the next iteration. When a sufficient step is found, it indicates that $m_k(s)$ is a good approximation, and the trust region can be increased ($\lambda$ decreased) to achieve faster convergence.

\subsection{Levenberg--Marquardt Method} \label{sec:levenberg_marquardt}

The Levenberg--Marquardt (LM) method \cite{madsen2004} is a damped Gauss--Newton method designed for solving nonlinear least-squares problems. It can be interpreted as a trust-region method in which the step is computed from a regularized Gauss--Newton system. The damping parameter, $\lambda$, controls the transition between the Gauss--Newton and steepest descent methods.\\

At iteration $k$, the LM step is obtained by solving the linear system
\begin{equation} \label{eq:lm_system}
    \left( J(x_k)^\top J(x_k) + \lambda I \right) s_k
    =
    - J(x_k)^\top r(x_k),
\end{equation}
which is obtained by substituting the quadratic model $m_k(s)$ in \cref{eq:mk} into \cref{eq:damped_problem}, and solving the minimization problem by setting the gradient with respect to $s$ to 0.\\

As $\lambda \to 0$, the system approaches the Gauss--Newton equations, \cref{eq:gauss_newton}, with $\alpha = 1$. For large $\lambda$, the step approaches,
\begin{equation*}
    s_k \simeq -\frac{1}{\lambda}J(x_k)^\top r(x_k),
\end{equation*}
which is the steepest descent direction, \cref{eq:steepest_descent}, with a small step lenght of $\alpha=\lambda^{-1}$.\\

The damping parameter must be initialized for the first iteration. The study in \cite{nielsen1999} states that the initial value should relate to the magnitude of the eigenvalues, and proposes
\begin{equation} \label{eq:init_lambda}
\lambda_0 = \tau \max \left( \operatorname{diag}(J(x_0)^\top J(x_0)) \right),
\end{equation}
where $\tau > 0$ is a small constant.\\

To evaluate the quality of a proposed step $s_k$, the LM method uses the gain ratio, defined as the ratio between the actual reduction in the objective function and the reduction predicted by the quadratic model,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gain_ratio}
\varrho =
\frac{f(x_k) - f(x_k + s_k)}
     {m_k(0) - m_k(s_k)}.
\end{equation}

The denominator can be computed from \cref{eq:mk},
\begin{align}
    m_k(0)-m_k(s_k) &= -s_k^\top J(x_k)^\top r(x_k) \,  - \frac{1}{2} s_k^\top J(x_k)^\top J(x_k) \, s_k \notag \\
    &= -\frac{1}{2} s_k^\top \Big(
    2 J(x_k)^\top r(x_k) + 
    \underbrace{(J(x_k)^\top J(x_k)+\lambda I)s_k}_{-J(x_k)^\top r(x_k)} 
    -\lambda s_k \Big)\notag \\
    &= \frac{1}{2} s_k^\top (\lambda s_k-J(x_k)^\top r(x_k)) \label{eq:mk_solve}.
\end{align}

Combining \cref{eq:gain_ratio,eq:mk_solve} yields a computationally easier expression for the gain ratio,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:gain_ratio_solve}
    \varrho = \frac{\|r(x_k)\|^2 - \|r(x_k+s_k)\|^2}
    {s_k^\top (\lambda s_k-J(x_k)^\top r(x_k))}.
\end{equation}
Note that $s_k^\top s_k$ and $-s_k^\top J(x_k)^\top r(x_k)$ are positive, such that the denominator is always positive.\\

If $\varrho > 0$, the step is accepted and the iterate is updated,
\[
x_{k+1} = x_k + s_k.
\]
The updated damping parameter, $\lambda_+$ is then decreased according to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dec_lambda}
\lambda_+ = 
\lambda \max \left( \frac{1}{3},\, 1 - (2\varrho - 1)^3 \right).
\end{equation}

If $\varrho \le 0$, the step is rejected, and the damping parameter is increased by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inc_lambda}
\lambda_+ = 2^{i+1}\lambda,
\end{equation}
where $i = 0,1,\dots,i_{\max}$ is the iteration counter until the step is accepted. This update strategy is demonstrated in \cite{nielsen1999}, and is shown to be a sufficient choice.\\

Intuitively, when $\varrho$ is positive, the quadratic model $m_k(s)$ provides a good approximation of the objective function, and the damping parameter is reduced so that the method behaves more like Gauss--Newton. When $\varrho$ is negative, the model is considered unreliable, and the damping parameter is increased, resulting in a smaller, more conservative step.


\subsubsection{Computing the step with SVD factorization}
\cref{eq:lm_system} can be solved by SVD factorization. Using,
\begin{equation*}
    J(x_k) = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^\top,
\end{equation*}
and performing some algebraic manipulation, the step can be computed as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sk_lm_solve}
    s_k = -V_k \, \text{diag}\left(\dfrac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_i^2 + \lambda} \right) \, U_k^\top r(x_k), 
\end{equation}
Where $\sigma_i$ are the singular values from $\Sigma_k$.\\

\cref{eq:sk_lm_solve} computes the LM step without computing $J^\top J$, which can be numerically unstable when the Jacobian is ill-conditioned.
