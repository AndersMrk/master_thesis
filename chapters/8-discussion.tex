\chapter{Discussion on Findings}
\label{chap:discussion}

This chapter discusses the thesis's main findings, the practical limitations of the proposed method, and directions for further work.

\section{Main Findings}

The results show that identifying multiple leaks from steady-state inlet and outlet measurements is a challenging problem. A significant change in leak parameters may produce only a small change in the outlet head and flow, making the inverse problem inherently ill-conditioned. Consequently, small changes in the measurements or model parameters can produce large changes in the estimated leak configuration. This was observed both through the large condition numbers in \autoref{chap:analysis_of_sol} and through the sensitivity analysis in \autoref{sec:mod_err}.\\

The difficulty increases rapidly with the number of leaks. As more leaks are introduced, the effect of each individual leak becomes harder to distinguish. This is reflected in the Jacobian, where the columns become increasingly aligned as $n$ increases. The measurements therefore contain less independent information about each additional leak, which makes the inverse problem increasingly close to singular.\\

The solver performs reliably for two and three leaks, although the results show increased dependence on the initial guess for three leaks. For four leaks, the solver remains useful but is no longer fully reliable. The true solution is not always recovered within the strict tolerance $\varepsilon=0.001$, and several test cases produce multiple solutions. However, many of these solutions are close to the true leak configuration, as shown in \autoref{sec:err_mag}. This indicates that the method can still provide useful localization information, even when exact identification fails.\\

For five leaks, the solver is not reliable. The true solution is rarely recovered, and most converged solutions are false according to the chosen tolerance. This is consistent with the conditioning analysis, where the condition number for five leaks is already on the order of $10^{12}$. Based on these results, four leaks appear to be close to the upper limit for reliable identification using only steady-state inlet and outlet measurements of head and flow. This limit is not absolute, since some configurations are easier to solve than others, but the general trend is clear. Identifying many simultaneous leaks from boundary measurements alone is unlikely to be feasible without additional information.\\

Even when the number of leaks exceeds what can be identified exactly, the method may still have value. As shown in \autoref{sec:n_not_known}, solving the problem with $\hat n=2$ or $\hat n=3$ often produced an estimated leak location close to a true leak. This suggests that the solver may be useful in an iterative localization strategy, where one leak is located and repaired before new measurements are collected.

\section{Practical Limitations}

A critical limitation is the assumption that the resistance coefficient \gls{theta} is known. In practice, \gls{theta} must often be estimated from pipe documentation, friction models, or calibration data. For old pipes without historical monitoring, leak-free calibration data may not be available. Since pipe roughness and effective resistance can change over time, the true value of \gls{theta} may differ significantly from its estimated value.\\

The sensitivity analysis showed that this is a serious issue. Small errors in \gls{theta} can lead to large errors in the estimated leak parameters. Although the residual may be used to search over possible values of \gls{theta}, it is uncertain whether this approach remains reliable when measurement noise and other model errors are present.\\

Measurement noise is also a major limitation. Even small relative measurement errors produced large variations in the estimated leak locations. Since the measurements represent steady-state conditions, random noise can be assumed to be limited, as the measurements can be obtained from the mean of many samples. Bias errors and deviations from the exact steady-state might still be present, limiting the method's practical performance.\\

Based on these results, the proposed solver likely has limited practical applicability in its current form. 

\section{Future Work}

The most important next step is to develop a method that is more robust to modeling errors and measurement noise. An interesting approach is to investigate whether pipe parameters, such as \gls{theta} and \gls{ell}, can be estimated alongside the leak parameters. This would improve practical applicability, but it would increase the dimension and difficulty of the inverse problem. It is not clear whether steady-state boundary measurements contain enough information for such simultaneous identification under modeling and measurement uncertainty.\\

Testing on real measurements is another important step. Experimental or field data would make it possible to evaluate whether the assumptions used in this work are sufficiently accurate in practice, and how large the actual modeling and measurement errors are.\\

An additional extension is to apply the method to small pipe networks with junctions. This would make the formulation more relevant to real water distribution systems, but would likely introduce additional identifiability challenges.\\

Finally, the results from \autoref{sec:n_not_known} indicate that solving the problem for several assumed values of $\hat n$ may help reduce the search distance. As briefly discussed in the section, combining results from several solver configurations could provide a more robust localization method for pipes with many leaks.
