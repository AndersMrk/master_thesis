\chapter{Literature Study}
\label{chap:literature}

This chapter presents the studied literature for this thesis. Leak localization is a broad field that includes hardware inspection, acoustic methods, transient tests, model-based estimation, and data-driven classification \cite{hu2021review}. This thesis has a narrower scope, focusing on the localization of several simultaneous leaks in a single pipeline using steady-state head and flow measurements at the pipe ends. The study is therefore limited to localization methods that use such measurements.\\

Some methods only support sequential leak localization. In these approaches, monitoring begins with the assumption that the pipeline has no leaks. It is further assumed that leaks do not occur simultaneously. Multiple leaks can then be treated as a sequence of single-leak localization problems, where the effect of each identified leak is accounted for before the next is identified. An example of this approach is presented in \cite{verde2005}. Such methods fall outside the scope of this research, and the literature review will be limited to methods that address simultaneous leaks.\\

Much of the existing research focuses on leak localization in pipe networks, whereas a smaller body of research addresses the localization of multiple leaks within a single pipeline. For completeness, \autoref{sec:lit:pipe_network} provides a brief introduction to research on network localization methods, with a focus on the differences towards single-pipe problems.\\

The identification of one or two leaks in a single pipeline using only endpoint sensors is well documented in the literature, whereas methods that address more than two concurrent leaks are rare. Most published methods use transient measurements, as discussed in \autoref{sec:lit:transients}, while the more limited work based on steady-state measurements is presented in \autoref{sec:lit:steadystate}. This section is most closely related to the work in this thesis and further discusses whether existing methods for two-leak identification can be extended to more leaks. \autoref{sec:lit:gap} summarizes the research gap and positions the contribution of this work.

\section{Leak Localization in Pipe Networks}
\label{sec:lit:pipe_network}

Leak localization in water distribution networks is usually formulated as a graph problem, with junctions as nodes and pipes as edges. Early inverse methods, as presented in \cite{pudar1992}, estimate leak areas at candidate nodes using hydraulic measurements. Modern reviews describe both model-based and data-driven methods that use pressure and flow data to identify likely leak regions in the network \cite{hu2021review}. A representative data-driven method is presented in \cite{hu2021multiscale}, where pipes are first clustered into leak zones and then classified into the most likely zone based on simulated pressure and flow residuals. A recent machine-learning method presented in \cite{zhang2026aignn} trains a neural network to reconstruct current pressures and predict leak-free pressures across the network. Leak localization is then performed by comparing pressure estimates, thereby identifying the candidate leaking pipe.\\

Most methods for leak localization in pipe networks output a node or edge in the network graph rather than an exact location along a pipe. A recent work presented in \cite{molno2024conditions} allows a leak to be located anywhere along an edge, which extends the localization problem to continuous positions along network edges. The analysis uses potential measurements at nodes and derives conditions on the network graph structure and sensor placement. Under these conditions, the analysis can limit the leak to a small set of possible locations, but it is limited to a single leak and does not address simultaneous leaks.\\

A steady-state result in \cite{molno2024parallel} studies a network of parallel pipes with only the two measured junction nodes. One operating point gives one plausible leak location in each pipe. Multiple operating points can then isolate the true leaking pipe because the true leak position remains invariant, while false pipe-leak hypotheses change with the operating point. This resembles the problem in this work in its use of multiple steady states and boundary measurements, except it identifies one leak across multiple pipelines, rather than several leaks in one pipeline.

\section{Transient-Based Pipeline Localization}
\label{sec:lit:transients}

Transient methods use pressure and flow measurements to characterize the hydraulic state as it changes. The inverse transient formulation in \cite{liggett1994} argues that a main disadvantage of steady-state measurements is that the friction coefficients in all pipes must be known accurately. Transient measurements contain more information than a single steady operating point and can be used to estimate leak and pipe parameters simultaneously. \\

Several different groups of methods use transient information. The input-output approach in \cite{diaz2021} is formulated for two simultaneous leaks. It uses a dynamic pipe model where the two leak positions and magnitudes are unknown parameters. A persistent excitation is applied, and the measured inlet and outlet signals are differentiated numerically to reconstruct the parameters that make the model consistent with the transient response.\\

Inverse transient analysis uses a direct matching principle. In \cite{nadian2025}, wavelet analysis first identifies reflected pressure-wave features to estimate the number of leaks and their approximate positions. These estimates define smaller search intervals, and the leak positions and sizes are then refined by selecting the transient model response that best matches the measured pressure signal. The method is developed and tested for one- and two-leak scenarios.\\

Frequency-domain methods use the shape of the transient response rather than the time signal directly. In \cite{wang2018}, the measured frequency response is modeled as a sum of contributions from several leaks. The method estimates leak positions and sizes for a specified number of leaks, with numerical results shown for two and three leaks. The extension in \cite{wang2019} removes the assumption of a fixed number of leaks by identifying one strong leak contribution at a time and using model selection to determine how many leak contributions to retain. This method is tested on cases with several leaks, including five leaks. A related frequency-domain method in \cite{kim2018} uses a multiple-leakage function and iterative optimization to estimate leak locations and sizes, with numerical results for three simultaneous leaks.\\

With respect to the leak counts studied in this thesis, the transient literature covers the two-leak case in several formulations and, in frequency-domain approaches, also includes numerical demonstrations with three or more leaks. However, this coverage relies on information unavailable in the steady-state setting considered here. The methods require either a transient event or a persistent excitation, along with a dynamic model describing how pressure waves propagate through the pipe. The main practical limitation is that the pressure signal must be measured at high sampling rates and with high signal quality to capture wave reflections. This makes transient methods sensitive to measurement noise and dependent on high-rate sensor systems. This is reflected in \cite{nadian2025}, where the pressure signal noise is analyzed explicitly, and the experimental setup uses pressure transmitters sampled at 1 kHz.

\section{Steady-State Pipeline Localization}
\label{sec:lit:steadystate}

Steady-state methods avoid active hydraulic excitation and can use data from ordinary operation. It is shown in \cite{verde2003} that input-output measurements at a single steady-state operating point are insufficient to identify two or more leaks in a pipeline. Extra information is needed through additional sensors, operating points, or constraints.\\

One way to add spatial information is to measure pressure along the pipeline, as presented in \cite{ostapkowicz2023}. A static two-leak model can then fit predicted pressures to intermediate pressure measurements or use pressure-gradient changes to identify leak-containing sections. This approach demonstrates that steady-state models can localize two leaks when the pressure profile is measured at multiple points, though it is not applicable to pipelines with only endpoint measurements.\\

A more closely related line of work presented in \cite{peralta2023} relies solely on endpoint measurements at several steady-state operating points. For one operating point, the equivalent single-leak location can be computed from the boundary data as
\begin{equation*}
L_{eq} =
\frac{h_0 - h_\ell - \theta \ell q_\ell^2}
{\theta \left(q_0^2 - q_\ell^2 \right)}.
\end{equation*}
For two leaks located at $L_1$ and $L_1+L_2$, the equivalent location has a fixed ordering:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lit_two_leak_order}
0 < L_1 < L_{eq} < L_1+L_2 < \ell.
\end{equation}
The value of $L_{eq}$ may change between operating points, but the ordering in \cref{eq:lit_two_leak_order} does not, and always separates the upstream leak from the downstream leak. This makes it possible to bound the search space and compare upstream and downstream descriptions of the same equivalent leak across several operating points. The method was improved in \cite{peralta2024}, and a case study is presented in \cite{peralta2025}, which shows both simulation and experimental results with a relative leak-location error of less than 7\%. A related Monte Carlo formulation in \cite{torres2024} uses the same equivalent-leak idea and returns probability distributions rather than a single point estimate. These studies are the closest predecessors to this thesis because they use simultaneous leaks, steady-state data, and endpoint measurements.\\

The mentioned two-leak identification methods do not scale directly to more leaks. \cref{eq:lit_two_leak_order} is specific to two leaks. For three leaks, the proof developed in \autoref{app:3_leak_eq_proof} shows that the equivalent location can move across the middle leak as the operating point changes. The ordering used by the two-leak methods no longer gives a consistent partition of the pipe. Based on this result, it seems difficult to extend the methods proposed in \cite{peralta2023,torres2024} to handle more than two leaks.

\section{Research Gap and Thesis Contribution}
\label{sec:lit:gap}

Based on the reviewed literature, it is evident that the problem of identifying multiple leaks along a single pipe has received limited attention. Most existing methods rely on transient data, while only a small number of studies consider the steady-state case. Among the published approaches, most are limited to identifying at most two concurrent leaks. Methods that support an arbitrary number of leaks typically depend on transient measurements and often assume discrete, fixed candidate leak locations. Only a few of these studies report results for scenarios involving more than two leaks.\\

This indicates a clear gap in the ability to identify more than two leaks using steady-state measurements. To the best of the author’s knowledge, the proposed method appears to be among the first to identify more than two concurrent leaks using only steady-state conditions, as demonstrated in \autoref{chap:results_performance}. The reviewed literature does not discuss the theoretical limits of the inverse problem as the number of leaks increases. This work addresses that gap through the analysis presented in \autoref{chap:analysis_of_sol}.
